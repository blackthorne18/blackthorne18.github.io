## Breaking Bad! ... Assumptions about bacteria

When I say ‘bacteria,’ most people think of disease. Plague. Stomach bugs. That one time someone didn’t refrigerate the potato salad. Or you just heard the first talk of the day and now have a personal vendetta against bacteria.
But what if I told you… bacteria aren't all bad. They’ve just had really bad PR.
They’re not just germs—they’re survivalists, engineers, ecosystem architects, and some of life’s oldest innovators.

They are here to reclaim their narrative. Tonight, I’m here to take them into their Reputation Era.

So the previous talk was about how bacteria are basically plotting our downfall. And that’s fair… they are.
But tonight, I’m here for their redemption arc.
I’m not saying bacteria deserve a PR team, but if they did—I’d be their agent.

Let’s start with your first roommates – no, not the ones who never took out the trash or played loud music all night. I’m talking about your *very first* roommates. The OGs. Bifidobacteria.

We were all babies once, right? Me too. I know a lot of people who were babies once—some of them still act like it—but that’s another talk.

When you’re born, your gut is a blank canvas. And Bifidobacteria are among the first to move in.
They don't just show up—they shape the whole neighbourhood.
They digest **human milk oligosaccharides** (HMOs), which are sugars in breast milk that you can’t digest.
Which begs the question: why are we even making them?
Turns out, HMOs evolved *specifically* to feed Bifidobacteria.
It’s one of the best examples we have of **co-evolution** between humans and microbes—our bodies literally designed food for bacteria that, in turn, protect us.

And protect us they do.

In one study, infants without sufficient Bifidobacteria showed delayed immune development, increased inflammation, and higher risk of infection.
But here’s the powerful part—when scientists restored Bifidobacteria using probiotics or transplants, their immune systems improved.

In neonatal intensive care units, adding Bifidobacteria has been shown to *reduce rates of deadly sepsis* in premature babies.
Without them, babies can grow up with hyperactive or confused immune responses.
Their presence isn’t just beneficial—it’s essential.
That’s not a germ. That’s a neonatal bodyguard.

And they don’t just help you digest—they acidify the gut, making the environment hostile to pathogens.
They’re like gut bouncers, nutritionists, and therapists rolled into one.

They are the perfect room mates, they clean your house, they cook you food, they keep you safe, and they care about your wellbeing. Gosh where were roommates like this when I was living in halls.

So where are the Bifidobacteria now?
As your diet changes, Bifidobacteria move out and other species take their place.
Your gut becomes a teeming metropolis, with specialists in every niche—digesting fibres, breaking down fats, balancing your mood, and yes, still kicking out the troublemakers.

There’s *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, producing butyrate, an anti-inflammatory compound that helps prevent colon cancer.
There’s *Akkermansia muciniphila*, hanging out in your mucus, strengthening your gut lining, and possibly helping with metabolic disorders.

But that’s just the domestic scene.

Let’s pan the camera way out—to  Earth’s most hostile corners
We’re talking boiling acid, crushing pressure, radioactive caves.
You’d think nothing could survive there. But bacteria? Bacteria *thrive* there.
These are chemotrophs—chemical eaters. And they don’t just survive—they transform their environments.

*Pyrolobus fumarii*, lives in deep-sea hydrothermal vents.
It grows best at 113°C—**hotter than boiling water**.
This bacterium thrives in metal-rich fluids, helps stabilise chemical gradients, and by oxidising hydrogen and reducing nitrate, it creates energy flows that *other microbes depend on*.
Without *Pyrolobus*, the vent ecosystem would **lack stability**, energy cycling would stall, and microbial diversity would collapse.
It’s like the infrastructure provider of the deep sea—a microbial civil engineer building invisible energy highways.

And then… there's *Desulforudis audaxviator*.
Living 2.8 kilometres underground in South Africa, it exists in total darkness, cut off from the surface for **millions of years**.
It survives on **hydrogen and sulfate** produced by the **radioactive decay of nearby rock**—no sunlight, no photosynthesis, no organic input.
It fixes nitrogen, processes carbon, and supports an entire (if tiny) ecosystem all by itself.
Scientists call it a **one-species biosphere**.
It’s not just a survivor—it’s a **proof of concept** that life *could* exist literally anywhere.

The lives of these chemotrophs are so cool and unique, imagine what it would be like dropping a camera into these caves and hydrothermal vents. We could film so many seasons of a reality show about them. We could call it Keeping up with the Chemotrophs.

These bacteria don’t just adapt to the world. They rewrite its chemistry.

Now, we’re not just watching them—we’re *learning* from them.

A lot of what we know about **evolution**, **genetics**, and **resilience** comes from these single-celled masterminds.

What have we learnt from them?

Let’s talk about genetic engineering. Quick show of hands, have people heard of CRISPR, glow in the dark mice that would have come up in the news, or Bt-Cotton?
Glow in the dark mice have been genetically engineered with a fluorescent gene that glows in the dark.
Bt-Cotton is a genetically engineered cotton crop with a gene from this one bacteria that keeps pests away.
So we got gene from source A and put it into source B. Must have been some clever scientist who created this tool right?

It was but not a human one. Bacteria created these tools.

Long ago, they evolved molecular scissors—restriction enzymes—to slice up viral invaders.
We picked them up and realised we could use them to do some DNA scrapbooking.
CRISPR is essentially bacterial scissors - it is their immune system, a way to cut up viruses trying to harm bacteria
Ligases are microbial glue that can stitch DNA back together.

We didn’t create genetic engineering. We borrowed it.
We just did the PR.

And if you’re worried about GMOs—don’t be. Bacteria have been modifying genes for billions of years. We're just late to the party.
Selective breeding has existed for millennia, but now we can edit crops with precision.
Take drought-resistant maize. You can crossbreed for decades, or you can tweak a gene in a season.
Same crop. Same benefits. Just less trial and error.

But bacteria don’t just edit DNA or eat sulfur. Some… *glow.* Like they’ve just gotten into a healthy relationship and are glowing from the inside.

Let me introduce *Aliivibrio fischeri*—a bioluminescent bacterium with a flair for performance.

It lives in the Hawaiian bobtail squid. And the squid *uses* it—intentionally.
At night, predators hunting from below look for shadows. The squid’s silhouette would be an easy target.
But its light organ—filled with *A. fischeri*—glows to match the moonlight.
**It erases its shadow.**
It’s not invisibility. It’s stealth mode, with style.
By glowing downward, the bacteria help the squid erase its shadow—turning a midnight snack into a ghost under the moonlight.

Now here’s the twist: the bacteria don’t glow alone.
They only light up when there are enough of them—**quorum sensing**.
If a few wander into a squid, they stay dark.
But once the population crosses a threshold, they flip the switch—like a coordinated rave.
It’s the same mechanism bacteria use to launch infections… except this time, it's used for stealth and partnership.

It’s like a group chat planning a trip. Everyone's vague at first. Someone sends a meme.
But unlike humans, bacteria don’t flake. They follow through. And when they do, they can do spectacular things.

The squid gets protection. The bacteria get food, shelter, and a fancy nightclub to hang out in. Now that’s what I call a healthy relationship
And it's been evolving for **millions of years**.

Let’s take a moment here.

**These creatures are invisible to the eye. Some live alone for centuries underground. Others help babies survive sepsis. Others build underwater lightshows with their squid partners.**
And still, we lump them all under “germs.”

So maybe it’s time we start seeing them not as pests to fear—but as partners in survival.

They digest our food. They build our ecosystems. They engineer our medicines.
They’ve helped us understand evolution, ecology, immunology, and even our own origin story.
They’re strange, ancient, and brilliant.

Bacteria aren’t just germs.
And they’ve been holding this planet together quietly for over 3 billion years.

Superhero movies are coming back into style so I want to pitch my idea for a bacterial superhero lineup.

So yes—bacteria are strange. Ancient. Often misunderstood.
But they are also brilliant, cooperative, and resilient.
And if they were running the planet before us, maybe we should be taking notes.
