## Genetic Mischief Makers: The Thrilling Tale of Jumping Genes

In this Pint of Science talk, I explored the growing global threat of antibiotic resistance and the hidden genetic players that drive it. While antibiotics transformed medicine after their discovery in the 1930s, resistant infections are now rising as bacteria acquire resistance genes carried on plasmids—small, mobile DNA elements that can move between cells through horizontal gene transfer. 

I introduced a new character to the story - plasmids, which are genetic “mischief makers,” capable of rapidly equipping bacteria with new traits, including the ability to withstand life-saving drugs, and highlight how their spread accelerates evolution far beyond what mutation alone could achieve.

My research focuses on understanding why some closely related bacteria accept particular plasmids while others reject them, with the long-term aim of identifying the genetic factors that make bacteria selective. By uncovering how to encourage this “pickiness,” we hope to limit the uptake of resistance plasmids and develop new strategies to curb the spread of antibiotic resistance in clinical settings.
