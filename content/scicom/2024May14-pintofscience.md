## Genetic Mischief Makers: The Thrilling Tale of Jumping Genes

### The threat of antibiotic resistance
I want you all to take a deep breath. Breathe in. Breathe out. Feels good doesn't it? For a lot of people with a bad cough, or a respiratory infection, it can be a challenge to enjoy a deep breath. Such infectious are most often caused by bacteria and we thought we knew how to deal with this. These bacterial infectious are getting harder to treat because we are fighting an invisible battle. A battle that we once thought had already been won. Now our fierce bacterial opponents are fighting back, and we are faced with a big challenge. How do we treat these infections?

Many years ago, back in prehistoric times - so the 1930s, we discovered antibiotics. They revolutionised medicine because bacterial infections that were once considered impossible to treat would now go away within weeks of being given an antibiotic. We thought we had won the battle against these infections. We sat back and relaxed, reading books, writing letters, sending pigeons - I don't know what people did before social media. But while we lounged, the bacteria were evolving, rapidly changing themselves and, under the watch of some helpless human observers, they became resistant to the commonly prescribed antibiotics. We had to scramble in the lab and produce new ones, but bacteria gain resistance faster than we can make new antibiotics.

Antibiotic resistance can spread easily within hospitals. They can affect upto half the people in an ICU increasing their risk the longer the treatment continues. Modern medicine is faced with a big challenge, how do you treat a bacterial infection when the bacteria is resistant to most antibiotics you would throw at it?

The good thing is it is very difficult for a bacteria to become resistant on it's own. It needs a little bit of help. It needs a sidekick in this battle - something to help it become resistant. This is where plasmids come in, and our fascinating story begins. Plasmids are what actually spread antibiotic resistance among bacteria. Like how bacteria spread from person to person causing infections, plasmids spread from bacteria to bacteria giving them the ability to resist antibiotics. What are plasmids?

### What are plasmids - aren't they so cool!
The world around us is teeming with life from the tiniest microbe to the innocous fruit fly to a French Poodle. Within the DNA that all these life forms are made up of, these is a hidden world of minuscule rebels, some of which are known as plasmids. They are tiny packets of genes that are floating around all by themselves waiting to infect a bacteria - sort of like viruses actually. If the bacterial DNA is a video game, plasmids are the DLC - the downloadable content. If the bacterial DNA is the Windows operating system, and a plasmid is a downloadable app like MS Teams.

Imagine a puddle with millions of bacteria floating around, going to the pub, going to concerts, living their life really. This puddle also contains thousands of plasmids floating around. These plasmids are like USB sticks and when the bump into a bacteria, they plug in and install new software into the bacteria. This allows the bacteria to do things it could not do before - it gives them superpowers! A bacteria that was once struggling to survive in the puddle because of dissolved antibiotics can now not only thrive but also digest the antibiotics as food! That's amazing for the bacteria but not so for us. Not always but often, this is how bacteria become resistant to antibiotics, not because they did anything themselves but because it took up a plasmid.

### What can plasmids do?
Plasmids can contain many genes that can give superpowers to the bacteria in which this plasmids has made a home. Some plasmids can have genes for surviving in toxic landscapes - imagine bacteria wearing hazmat suits stitched by plasmids and walking into Chernobyll. Some can have genes for making bacteria stronger - they can give them the strength and resources to survive in harsh environments. Some can have genes for making bacteria swim faster - they coach the bacteria and train them to become the next Michael Phelps. They are fascinating to study and you never know what kind of a plasmid you'll end up finding in the wild.

### Why are plasmids around?
The thing about plasmids is that they are actually uninvited guests. When there are antibiotics in the environment of the bacteria, the plasmids provide a benefit. But when there are no antibiotics, they provide no other advantage to the bacteria - they are basically freeloader and mischeif makers. They don't care about bacteria - they're on a quest for survival and self-interest, creating genetic mischief in the world of bacteria. Plasmids are trying to create their own story and write their own scripts in a world of bacterial Hitchcock's, Nolan's and Tarrantino's.

Evolution is painstakingly slow and takes small steps to change and adapt to the environment. When it might have taken millions of generations for a bacteria to become resistant to antibiotics or becoming a better swimmer like Michael Phelps, plasmids can help bacteria do this in a single generation. Imagine if humans had plasmids, which we unfortunately don't - sorry to burst the bubble. But if we did, rather than taking millions of years, for our ape ancestors to start walking, it would have taken them just a few decades. These genetic mishcief makers are the architects of genetic innovation, rewiring the bacterial genome to create new possibilities.

### The spread of plasmids
Once a plasmid enters a population it doesn't take long before they spread to the whole population. It's like an undergrad up at the university finding a pirated copy online of Fast and Furious 20 before its released - you know that by the end of the week everyone on campus has already watched it. It spreads like wildfire.

These plasmids are also capable of spreading very easily from one bacteria to another by a process called horizontal gene transfer! What does that mean? Plasmids spread by contact. Here is where I tell you about the birds and the bees. So you have one bacteria, that comes into contact with another bacteria. They touch. The bacteria with the plasmid creates a sort of syringe and injects the plasmid into the bacteria. If you're beeing PG13 about it, it's basically a handshake. That is so cool isn't it! Bacteria passing genes and their superpowers around by shaking hands. While humans pass on their genes from parent to offspring, bacteria can also pass on genes through these plasmids to their neighbours, even from other species as well.

Just imagine if we could do that – if we could pick up skills or powers just by sharing a friendly handshake! It would be cool to bump into F1 legend Lewis Hamilton and suddenly you can drive from the Ship and Mitre to your home in 4.5s. Or you shake hands with Ian McKellen and you'd be performing at the West End. You can't control who bumps into you. Bacteria can't either and they don't know what plasmids and genes they will pick up from another source. The results are often unexpected. It's like if you shook hands with Gordan Ramsay, rather than being able to cook really well, all you can do is go around calling people an Idiot Sandwhich.

### What we do
For the longest time, we thought that bacteria have to accept what plasmid they are dumped with and have no say in the matter. However we are starting to realise there is more to the story. We have seen that bacteria that are closely related can be very picky about the same plasmid. It's like when everyone in the family enjoys The Traitors but you just want to watch Love Island.

We are trying to figure out what preferences bacteria have when choosing plasmids. Some bacteria might prefer plasmids that are small, or don't move around too much. But it's more nuanced, there are probably specific genes on plasmids that bacteria do not like and will never accept a plasmid with that gene. Why does this matter?

As I have been talking about, plasmids can give bacteria superpowers. One of them, unfortunately for us, is resistance to antibiotics. Resistance to that life saving drug that has been clearing bacterial infections since the 1930's. Plasmids spread this resistance.

Imagine if we could tweak the bacteria, modify them, make tiny changes so that the bacteria start disliking these plasmids. If we can make the bacteria picky, we can ensure that they won't take up plasmids with antibiotic resistance and thus will stop the spread.

What we actually do in the lab is this. We have a family of very closely related bacteria in the lab that we study. We give each of them the same plasmid to see whether or not they accept it. This is directly measuring how picky the bacterial family is for this plasmid. In this case, three of the members of the family accept the plasmid and two don't. Now that we have a difference in the features of these bacteria, we can dive into the DNA and take a look. When we read the DNA that is in these bacteria, we might find a clear difference between the bacteria that can accept the plasmid and ones that do not. Say for example, the orange gene helps the bacteria accept a plasmid while having a pink gene makes the bacteria picky. That would be great. In reality, it's a lot more tedious where there are millions of differences between the strains. I am going to be spending the next few years working to just figure this out - sort of like a preschooler with a remote control car, take things out putting things back in and playing around with the DNA until I figure out which part of the bacteria's DNA makes it picky when choosing plasmids. If we know what this, we can start out with a bacteria that likes the plasmid, simple insert this new bit of DNA into the bacteria, and voila, we have a picky bacteria!

We're trying to turn bacteria into a grumpy interviewer rejecting every plasmid that appears for the interview. Which is great for us, cause this will put an end to the spread of antibiotic resistance. A word of caution, what I just described in a few minutes is decades of work but it is very promising and it will be revolutionary when it happens. We are trying to influence bacteria and get them to help us stop the spread of resistance.

### To summarise
Bacteria can cause a range of infections and when these bacteria are resistant to antibiotics, it can be difficult to treat.
Resistance to antibiotics is an evil power that is spread amongst bacteria through plasmids.
Plasmids are small packets of genes that can give bacteria superpowers and often something sinister such as antibiotic resistance.
Plasmids can spread from one bacteria to another through a handshake and they can do this fast.
We have found that sometimes bacteria are picky and can reject a plasmid.
If we can find out what a bacteria's criteria is for picking a plasmid, we can influence bacteria that cause infections to stop taking up plasmids that spread resistance.
We can convince them to stop the spread of antibiotic resistance and make infections easier to treat and reducing the burden on the healthcare industry.

I want you all to take another deep breath. This time picture the bacteria shaking hands, swapping genes and spreading evil and smile knowing that the evil reign of antibiotic resistance will soon come to an end.

It's time to make antibiotics great again!
